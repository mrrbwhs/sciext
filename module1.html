1.1 – How have philosophical arguments influenced the development of modern scientific research?
  • 1.1.1 – explore epistemology and alternative ways of knowing, for example the development of navigation
  • 1.1.2 – describe the influence of empiricism on scientific inquiry
  • 1.1.3 – compare induction and deduction with reference to scientific inquiry
  • 1.1.4 – assess parsimony/Occam’s razor and its influence on the development of science
  • 1.1.5 – analyse the importance of falsifiability in scientific research
  • 1.1.6 - evaluate the significance of confirmation bias, including theory-dependence of observation
  • 1.1.7 – use historical examples to evaluate the contribution of cultural observational knowledge and its relationship to science, including:
    • 1.1.7.1 - post – 49000 BCE, exemplified by Aboriginal cultures
    • 1.1.7.2 - pre – 1500 CE, exemplified by Greek and Egyptian cultures and those of the Asia region
  • 1.1.8 - select one example from the following list to analyse the paradigm shift and how evidence is used to support new theories to explain phenomena and their consequences:
    • Lavoisier and oxygen
    • Einstein and general relativity
    • Wegener and continental drift, leading to plate tectonics
    • McClintock and transposable elements, commonly known as ‘jumping genes’
1.2 – What are the benefits and drawbacks of qualitative and quantitative observations?
  • 1.2.1 – carry out a practical activity to qualitatively and quantitatively describe, for example:
    • 1.2.1.1 – microscopic images of a variety of cells
    • 1.2.1.2 – geological strata in rock faces and road cuttings
    • 1.2.1.3 – an object falling due to gravity
    • 1.2.1.4 – characteristics of acids and bases
  • 1.2.2 – analyse the quantitative data from the following information sources, including but not limited to:
    • 1.2.2.1 – digital images and hand-drawn diagrams of cells
    • 1.2.2.2 – geological succession obtained from rock strata
    • 1.2.2.3 – graphs of results obtained from observations of an object falling due to gravity
    • 1.2.2.4 – data showing the pH of acids and bases
  • 1.2.3 – evaluate the differences between qualitative and quantitative observations and data and where these are used
1.3 – How does primary data provide evidence for further investigation?
  • 1.3.1 – use data gathered to plan a practical investigation to:
    • 1.3.1.1 – pose further questions that will be investigated
    • 1.3.1.2 – discuss the role of variables
    • 1.3.1.3 – determine the independent and dependent variables
    • 1.3.1.4 – formulate a hypothesis that links the independent and dependent variables
    • 1.3.1.5 – describe at least three variables that should be controlled in order to increase the validity of the investigation
  • 1.3.2 – develop a method to collect primary data for a practical investigation by:
    • 1.3.2.1 – describing how to change the independent variable
    • 1.3.2.2 – determining the characteristics of the measurements that will form the dependent variable
    • 1.3.2.3 – describing how the data will be collected
    • 1.3.2.4 – describing how the controlled variables will be made consistent
    • 1.3.2.5 – describing how risks can be minimised
  • 1.3.3 – evaluate how observation is limited by the observational tools available, including but not limited to:
    • 1.3.3.1 – observing the Universe
    • 1.3.3.2 – digital versus analogue technologies
1.4 – How does the collection and presentation of primary data affect the outcome of a scientific investigation?
  • 1.4.1 – carry out the planned practical investigation, above, to collect primary data
  • 1.4.2 – apply conventions for collecting and recording observations to qualitatively and quantitatively analyse the primary data, including but not limited to:
    • 1.4.2.1 – tabulation
    • 1.4.2.2 – graphing
    • 1.4.2.3 – visual representations
    • 1.4.2.4 – digital representations
  • 1.4.3 – compare the usefulness of observations recorded in the initial practical activity with the primary data gathered in this planned practical investigation
1.5 – How do conclusions drawn from the interpretation of primary data promote further scientific investigation?
  • 1.5.1 – draw conclusions from the analysis of the primary data collected in the practical investigation
  • 1.5.2 – evaluate the process of drawing conclusions from the primary data collected
  • 1.5.3 – assess the findings of the scientific investigation in relation to the findings of other related investigations
  • 1.5.4 – assess the need to make further observations by gathering data about other phenomena arising from the practical investigation

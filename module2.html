2.1 – What are the processes needed for developing a scientific research question and initial hypothesis?
    • 2.1.1 – conduct an initial literature search to identify the potential use of a contemporary, relevant publicly available data set
    • 2.1.2 – develop a scientific research question from the literature search
    • 2.1.3 – formulate an initial scientific hypothesis based on the scientific research question
    • 2.1.4 – evaluate the resources associated with the initial scientific hypothesis in terms of:
      • 2.1.4.1 – scope to perform an investigation to obtain primary data
      • 2.1.4.2 – availability of secondary-sourced data
      • 2.1.4.3 – availability of relevant publicly available data set(s)
      • 2.1.4.4 – reliability and validity
      • 2.1.4.5 – assessing the current state of the theory, concept, issue or problem
    • 2.1.5 – assess the process involved in the development of a scientific research question and relevant hypothesis
2.2 – How is scientific research planned, based on a relevant hypothesis?
  • 2.2.1 – conduct a detailed literature review to support the validity, significance and appropriateness of the scientific research question
  • 2.2.2 – formulate a final scientific hypothesis
  • 2.2.3 – develop the rationale and possible outcomes for the chosen scientific research
  • 2.2.4 – develop a detailed plan to investigate the hypothesis, including:
    • 2.2.4.1 – overall strategy
    • 2.2.4.2 – methodology
    • 2.2.4.3 – data analysis
    • 2.2.4.4 – representation and communication
    • 2.2.4.5 – timelines
    • 2.2.4.6 – benchmarks
  • 2.2.5 – critically analyse the research plan to refine and amend appropriately
  • 2.2.6 – employ accepted referencing protocols, for example:
    • 2.2.6.1 – APA
    • 2.2.6.2 – Harvard
    • 2.2.6.3 – MLA
2.3 – How is an appropriate methodology developed to collect valid and reliable data?
  • 2.3.1 – assess and evaluate uncertainty in experimental evidence, including:
    • 2.3.1.1 – systematic errors
    • 2.3.1.2 – random errors
  • 2.3.2 – assess and evaluate the use of errors in:
    • 2.3.2.1 – mathematical calculations involving degrees of uncertainty
    • 2.3.2.2 – graphical representations from curves of best fit
  • 2.3.3 – compare quantitative and qualitative research methods, including:
    • 2.3.3.1 – design of method
    • 2.3.3.2 – gathering of data
    • 2.3.3.3 – analysis of data
  • 2.3.4 – investigate various methods used to obtain large data sets, for example:
    • 2.3.4.1 – remote sensing
    • 2.3.4.2 – streamed data
  • 2.3.5 – propose a suitable method to gather relevant data (including large data sets if appropriate)
2.4 – How is data processed so that it is ready for analysis?
  • 2.4.1 – investigate appropriate methods for processing, recording, organising and storing data using modern technologies
  • 2.4.2 – conduct a practical investigation to obtain qualitative and quantitative data, applying appropriate processing methods
  • 2.4.3 – assess the impact of making a large data set public, for example:
    • 2.4.3.1 – Large Hadron Collider
    • 2.4.3.2 – Kepler Telescope
    • 2.4.3.3 – Human Genome Project
  • 2.4.4 – conduct an investigation to access and obtain relevant publicly available data set(s) associated with the proposed hypothesis
